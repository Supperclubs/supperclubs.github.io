Session #2

What do we really mean when we talk about love?

Wholeness, longing, and the courage to love without dissolving.

February 2026

The Question

Love is the word we use for everything from desire to devotion, from friendship to obsession. We wanted to go back to two very different sources—one ancient, one modern—and ask what love actually is. Aristophanes tells us we are halves searching for our other half. Rilke tells us love is not merging but the work of two solitudes meeting. Are these contradictions, or two sides of the same truth?

What We Read

Primary text

The Speech of Aristophanes (Symposium, 189c–193d)

Plato, translated by Benjamin Jowett

Letter

Letter 6, from Letters to a Young Poet

Rainer Maria Rilke

From the Text: Aristophanes' Myth

Primeval man was round, his back and sides forming a circle; and he had four hands and the same number of feet, one head with two faces, looking opposite ways, set on a round neck and precisely alike; also four ears, two privy members, and the remainder to correspond.

He could walk upright as men now do, backwards or forwards as he pleased, and he could also roll over and over at a great pace, turning on his four hands and four feet, eight in all, like tumblers going over and over with their legs in the air; this was when he wanted to run fast.

Terrible was their might and strength, and the thoughts of their hearts were great, and they made an attack upon the gods. Zeus cut them in two, like a sorb-apple which is halved for pickling. After the division the two parts of man, each desiring his other half, came together, and throwing their arms about one another, entwined in mutual embraces, longing to grow into one.

And the reason is that human nature was originally one and we were a whole, and the desire and pursuit of the whole is called love.

— Plato, Symposium (c. 385 BC), trans. Benjamin Jowett

From the Text: Rilke on Love

In his sixth letter to the young poet Franz Xaver Kappus, written from Rome in December 1903, Rilke turns to the subject of love with a startling claim: that love is not the collapse of two people into one, but the meeting of two solitudes. He writes that young people are bad at loving because they throw themselves at each other, scattering themselves in a kind of generous waste. Real love, Rilke argues, requires something harder—the willingness to stand beside another person without losing your own shape.

For one human being to love another: that is perhaps the most difficult of all our tasks, the ultimate, the last test and proof, the work for which all other work is but preparation.

Love is a high inducement to the individual to ripen, to become world, to become world for himself for another's sake; it is a great exacting claim upon him, something that chooses him out and calls him to vast things.

— Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet, Letter 7 (1903), trans. M.D. Herter Norton

Note: Rilke returns to love across several letters. The passage above comes from Letter 7, which continues themes begun in Letter 6. Both were part of our reading.

Questions to Sit With

  • Aristophanes says love is the desire to become whole again. Rilke says love requires remaining separate. Can both be true at the same time?
  • Do you believe in the idea of a "missing half"? Has that metaphor helped or harmed the way you approach relationships?
  • Rilke says young people are bad at love. What does he mean? Is love something you get better at, like a craft?
  • Aristophanes' myth includes three original genders—male, female, and androgynous. What does it mean that one of the oldest stories about love is also a story about the diversity of desire?
  • Is there a difference between wanting someone and wanting to be completed by them?
  • Rilke describes love as "the work for which all other work is but preparation." What kind of preparation is he talking about?
  • The myth ends with Zeus threatening to split us again if we misbehave. What does it mean that even in the story, love is precarious—something that can be taken away?

What We Found

  • The Aristophanes myth provoked more disagreement than expected. Some found the "other half" idea beautiful; others found it claustrophobic—the idea that you are fundamentally incomplete without someone else.
  • Rilke's vision of love as the meeting of two solitudes was harder to sit with. It asks more of you: to love without collapsing, to remain yourself inside intimacy. Several people admitted they weren't sure they'd managed that.
  • A question that stayed with us: "If the pursuit of wholeness is love, what do you call it when you stop pursuing and simply sit with someone in your incompleteness?"
