Session #2

Should future generations have political representation today?

Democracy, time horizons, and who speaks for tomorrow.

February 2026

The Question

Climate policy, national debt, AI governance—so many of the decisions we make today will shape lives that haven't begun yet. But those future people can't vote, can't lobby, can't protest. Should our political systems account for them? And if so, how? We wanted to explore what it means to take the future seriously as a political constituency.

What We Read

Article

Longtermism: An Offer You Can't Refuse?

Kieran Setiya, Boston Review

Paper

Representing Future Generations

Gustaf Arrhenius

Questions We Explored

  • Can you represent someone who doesn't exist yet?
  • Is there a moral difference between harming current people and failing to benefit future ones?
  • Would a "Future Generations Commissioner" actually work, or is it just a feel-good gesture?
  • How far into the future should our moral obligations extend?

What We Found

  • Most of us arrived skeptical of longtermism but left more sympathetic—not to the utilitarian calculus, but to the basic intuition that we owe something to the future beyond what we owe ourselves.
  • The idea of a Future Generations Commissioner (like Wales has) was compelling, though we debated whether advisory bodies without real power can make a difference.
  • A recurring theme: the tension between democratic legitimacy (people vote on what matters to them now) and intergenerational justice (some things are too important to leave to the present).
