Session #1

Should everyone have the right to vote, regardless of knowledge or engagement?

Epistocracy, equality, and the promise of universal suffrage.

January 2026

The Question

We take universal suffrage as a given—the bedrock of democracy. But what if the right to vote came with responsibilities? What if political decisions were better made by those who study the issues? We wanted to sit with the discomfort of that question: is there a tension between equality and competence in governance, and if so, how should we navigate it?

What We Read

Book excerpt

Against Democracy (Chapter 1)

Jason Brennan

Questions We Explored

  • Is voting a right, a privilege, or a responsibility?
  • Who gets to decide what counts as "political knowledge"?
  • Could epistocracy ever avoid becoming a tool of the powerful?
  • What do we owe to democracy even when it fails us?

What We Found

  • The group was more sympathetic to epistocratic arguments than expected—but only in the abstract. In practice, everyone agreed the risks of gatekeeping outweigh the benefits.
  • We kept returning to the idea that democracy's value isn't just instrumental (good outcomes) but intrinsic (dignity, equality, belonging).
  • A question that stayed with us: "If you wouldn't trust anyone to decide who gets to vote, why would you trust anyone to decide who doesn't?"
