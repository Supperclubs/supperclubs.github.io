Session #3

What can you know for certain?

Doubt, certainty, and the one thing you cannot question.

March 2026

The Question

In 1637, a young Frenchman locked himself in a heated room in Germany and decided to doubt everything. His senses. His reasoning. The physical world. He stripped it all away—and found, in the rubble, one thing that could not be doubted. That moment became the foundation of modern philosophy. We wanted to read Descartes slowly, follow his demolition, and ask whether the cogito still holds—and what it means for how we think about knowledge, certainty, and the limits of our own minds.

What We Read

Primary text

Discourse on the Method, Part II: The Four Rules

René Descartes, translated by John Veitch

Primary text

Discourse on the Method, Part IV: The Cogito

René Descartes, translated by John Veitch

From the Text: The Heated Room

I was then in Germany, attracted thither by the wars in that country, which have not yet been brought to a termination; and as I was returning to the army from the coronation of the emperor, the setting in of winter arrested me in a locality where, as I found no society to interest me, and was besides fortunately undisturbed by any cares or passions, I remained the whole day in seclusion, with full opportunity to occupy my attention with my own thoughts.

Because we have all to pass through a state of infancy to manhood, and have been of necessity, for a length of time, governed by our desires and preceptors, I farther concluded that it is almost impossible that our judgments can be so correct or solid as they would have been, had our reason been mature from the moment of our birth, and had we always been guided by it alone.

— Descartes, Discourse on the Method, Part II (1637), trans. John Veitch

From the Text: The Four Rules

The first was never to accept anything for true which I did not clearly know to be such; that is to say, carefully to avoid precipitancy and prejudice, and to comprise nothing more in my judgement than what was presented to my mind so clearly and distinctly as to exclude all ground of doubt.

The second, to divide each of the difficulties under examination into as many parts as possible, and as might be necessary for its adequate solution.

The third, to conduct my thoughts in such order that, by commencing with objects the simplest and easiest to know, I might ascend by little and little, and, as it were, step by step, to the knowledge of the more complex.

And the last, in every case to make enumerations so complete, and reviews so general, that I might be assured that nothing was omitted.

— Descartes, Discourse on the Method, Part II (1637), trans. John Veitch

From the Text: I Think, Therefore I Am

But immediately upon this I observed that, whilst I thus wished to think that all was false, it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be somewhat; and as I observed that this truth, I THINK, THEREFORE I AM, was so certain and of such evidence that no ground of doubt, however extravagant, could be alleged by the sceptics capable of shaking it, I concluded that I might, without scruple, accept it as the first principle of the philosophy of which I was in search.

I thence concluded that I was a substance whose whole essence or nature consists only in thinking, and which, that it may exist, has need of no place, nor is dependent on any material thing; so that "I," that is to say, the mind by which I am what I am, is wholly distinct from the body, and is even more easily known than the latter, and is such, that although the latter were not, it would still continue to be all that it is.

— Descartes, Discourse on the Method, Part IV (1637), trans. John Veitch

Watch: Do You Know You Exist?

James Zucker's TED-Ed lesson traces Descartes' argument from radical doubt to the cogito—a useful companion before or after the reading.

Questions to Sit With

  • Descartes says our beliefs formed before we could evaluate them. How much of what you believe was chosen, and how much was installed?
  • He chose to do this alone, in a room, in winter. He trusted one mind in solitude over many minds together. When have you thought most clearly—alone or with others?
  • Rule 1: accept nothing unless it is so clear and distinct that there is no possible ground for doubt. What in your life survives that standard?
  • Senses deceive. Reasoning fails. Dreams feel real. Which of Descartes' three grounds for doubt is most convincing to you? Which weakest?
  • He goes from "thinking is occurring" to "I exist." But who is the "I"? Could there be thinking without a thinker?
  • Ubuntu says "I am because we are"—almost an inversion of the cogito. Is solitary reasoning the deepest kind of knowledge, or the most limited?
  • If an AI processes text, does it therefore exist? Descartes drew the line between humans and machines at thinking. Does his line still hold?
  • Conspiracy thinking is Descartes' doubt without the exit. What separates productive scepticism from destructive scepticism?

What We Found

  • The discussion guide structured the evening around five close readings from Parts II and IV. Reading the four rules aloud, they sound like common sense. Applied honestly, the group realized, they destroy everything.
  • The AI question split the table. Some argued that if a machine can reason, it meets Descartes' criterion. Others said the cogito requires not just thinking but knowing that you think—a self-awareness we can't verify from the outside.
  • Several people were drawn to the comparison between Descartes' method of doubt and modern conspiracy thinking: both start by questioning everything, but Descartes found a floor and rebuilt. The question of what separates productive from destructive scepticism stayed with us.

Go Further

The Discourse is just the beginning. Descartes expanded the argument in the Meditations—fuller and more dramatic.
